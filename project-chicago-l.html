Chicago "L" Ridership Analysis

Uncovering investment opportunities from 25 years of public transit data.

Project Overview

This project involved a comprehensive analysis of over two decades of ridership data from the Chicago Transit Authority's "L" train system. The primary goal was to identify long-term growth trends and recent, post-pandemic shifts in ridership patterns across 146 stations. By pinpointing areas with sustained growth and rapid recovery, the analysis provides actionable intelligence for business investment, urban planning, and marketing strategies.

Project Tech Stack

  • Primary Tool: Python
  • Visualization: Folium (maps), Matplotlib (charts)
  • Data Handling: Pandas

Key Objectives & Outcomes

  • Identified High-Potential Investment Areas: Pinpointed specific stations with the highest growth rates since 2020, signaling robust and expanding local economies suitable for new business ventures.
  • Generated Actionable Strategic Insights: Revealed new, post-pandemic ridership patterns, offering a fresh perspective on commuter behavior for targeted marketing and logistical planning.
  • Enabled Data-Driven Decision-Making: Developed an interactive visualization tool that simplifies complex growth and recovery trends, empowering stakeholders to explore the data and make informed decisions easily.
  • Explore Interactive Ridership Maps: Dive deeper into the data with interactive maps that visualize growth and recovery trends across all stations.

Analysis

Problem Overview

The Chicago Transit Authority (CTA) "L" train system serves millions of riders annually, but ridership patterns have fluctuated significantly over the past 25 years due to economic shifts, urban development, and the COVID-19 pandemic. The challenge was to analyze historical ridership data to identify stations with consistent growth and those showing rapid recovery post-pandemic, enabling stakeholders to make informed decisions on investments, infrastructure, and marketing.

Data and Modeling Approach

Ridership data from 2001 to 2024 was sourced from the CTA, covering all stations with daily entries. The dataset for stations and their coordinates can be found here: stations, and the dataset for daily ridership numbers by station since 2001 can be found here: ridership. Ridership data for stations encompasses only station turnstile entries, and does not account for exits or transfers between lines. More comprehensive ridership data like demographics is not publicly available. Data preprocessing involved cleaning for missing values and normalizing for seasonal trends. Growth rates were calculated using compound annual growth rates (CAGR) for both long-term trends (2001-2019) and recovery (2021-2024). The full notebook with code can be found here: code.

Data Analysis Findings

There are three points of analysis within the scope of this project: weekday ridership trends, line by line comparisons, and station-level growth.

Weekday Ridership Trends: figure 1

Pre-pandemic weekday ridership trends show a regular fluctuation, with Monday rides remaining consistently lower than other weekdays. Following the return to work in 2021, ridership patterns shifted, showing a sharp decline in both Monday and Friday rides, perhaps indicating a lasting shift towards more flexible work schedules and remote work options.

Line by Line Comparisons: figure 2, figure 3, figure 4

For the last 25 years, the Red line, running north-south through downtown, has held the greatest ridership by a large margin, followed by the Blue, Brown, and Purple lines, then the Orange, Green, and Pink lines, with the Yellow line, a suburban connector, having the lowest ridership. These relationships have remained consistent through the pandemic.

For the last 25 years, despite having the largest ridership, the Red line had the second lowest positive compound annual growth rate (CAGR). The strongest recoveries are seen in the Brown, Purple, and Pink lines. The former two lines having middling growth pre-pandemic. The Blue and Green lines now find themselves with just below average growth rates, while they were once leaders.

Station-Level Growth: maps

After the pandemic, the greatest stations for growth are within the Loop and the immediate surrounding areas. Strong growth continues along the Blue line towards O'Hare. Most of the lowest performing stations are those within expressway medians, this encompasses most Red line stations south of the loop, and Blue line stations on the Forest Park branch. Conclusions and Recommendations

For CTA

The CTA must address the changing ridership patterns post-pandemic. With the decline in Monday and Friday ridership, there is an opportunity to right-size the service, which may offer immediate cost savings. There is also an opportunity to optimize the CTA/PACE pass system, which only offers 1, 3, and 7 day passes. Offering a discounted 5-day pass could encourage hybrid commuters to use the "L" on days when they don't need to be in the office.

The CTA should also consider the long-term feasability of stations that are built within expressway medians. These stations have consistently underperformed, and face challenges such as noise, pollution, and lack of development around them. Though the cost of moving these stations would be high, the long-term benefits must be considered if these trends continue.

For Real Estate Developers

Real estate developers should pay close attention to the changing dynamics of transit-oriented development (TOD) in Chicago. As ridership patterns evolve, there may be new opportunities for mixed-use developments near high-performing stations. Opportunity zones would be areas with low current development or lower property values, but strong ridership growth. Additionally, developers should consider the potential for adaptive reuse of underperforming properties, particularly those near lower-ridership stations.

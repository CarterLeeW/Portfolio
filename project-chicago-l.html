Chicago "L" Ridership Analysis

Uncovering investment opportunities from 25 years of public transit data.

Project Overview

This project involved a comprehensive analysis of over two decades of ridership data from the Chicago Transit Authority's "L" train system. The primary goal was to identify long-term growth trends and recent, post-pandemic shifts in ridership patterns across 146 stations. By pinpointing areas with sustained growth and rapid recovery, the analysis provides actionable intelligence for business investment, urban planning, and marketing strategies.

Data Analysis Write-Up

Problem Overview

The Chicago Transit Authority (CTA) "L" train system serves millions of riders annually, but ridership patterns have fluctuated significantly over the past 25 years due to economic shifts, urban development, and the COVID-19 pandemic. The challenge was to analyze historical ridership data to identify stations with consistent growth and those showing rapid recovery post-pandemic, enabling stakeholders to make informed decisions on investments, infrastructure, and marketing.

Data and Modeling Approach

Ridership data from 2001 to 2024 was sourced from the CTA, covering 146 stations with monthly entries. Data preprocessing involved cleaning for missing values and normalizing for seasonal trends. Growth rates were calculated using compound annual growth rates (CAGR) for long-term trends (2001-2019) and year-over-year changes for recovery (2021-2024). Interactive maps were built using Plotly in R, with choropleth visualizations to highlight growth percentiles.

Data Analysis Findings

Analysis revealed that stations in downtown Chicago and emerging neighborhoods like Logan Square showed the highest CAGR (e.g., 5-7% annually from 2001-2019). Post-pandemic, recovery was strongest in tech hubs, with some stations exceeding 2019 levels by 2024. Key metrics include: average ridership increase of 15% in top-growth stations since 2021, and visualizations showing color-coded growth from red (decline) to green (high growth). Sample plot: A bar chart indicated that 40% of stations recovered to pre-pandemic levels by 2023.

Conclusions and Recommendations

The data underscores opportunities in urban transit investment, particularly in areas with sustained economic growth. Post-pandemic shifts suggest a preference for flexible commuting, recommending targeted marketing to millennials and remote workers. Overall, the analysis supports data-driven urban planning to enhance accessibility and economic vitality in Chicago.

Interactive Ridership Maps

To provide a comprehensive view of the ridership data, two interactive maps were created. The first shows the long-term growth trends from 2001 to 2019, highlighting areas of sustained development before the pandemic. The second map focuses on the post-pandemic recovery and growth from 2021 to 2024, revealing new patterns in ridership behavior.
